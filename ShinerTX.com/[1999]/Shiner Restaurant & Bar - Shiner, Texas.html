The Shiner Restaurant & Bar is located in the historic and award-winning Green Building, in the center of downtown Shiner, Texas, at the corner of Seventh Street and Highway 90A. Shiner is only 80 miles from Austin and just over 100 miles from San Antonio or Houston ... we're 20 miles south of IH-10 at the intersection of Texas Highways 90A and 95.

The building is ornate and stately, with its 14-foot-high embossed tin ceilings, six-inch thick walls, eight-foot picture windows, granite columns, wood carvings, stained-glass windows, and a corner entrance with a tiled star inside a circle with the year "1911."

Elegant Dining Room

The spacious Dining Room, which seats 64 guests, is elegantly appointed in warm green and gold. Once a local pharmacy, it is filled with beautiful cherry wood cabinets. Inside the showcases is a treasure-trove of local Texas history, including relics from the Half Moon Post Office, the Indians, Texas Rangers, the Wagner Hospital, Kaspar Wire Works, the Civil War, Texas Independence and World War II.

Remembered are heroes and scoundrels, villains and men of honor. Items are both tribute and memorial to the past: Shiner National Bank and Civil War currency, Shiner's four major league baseball players, guns and hatchets, knives and swords, saddles and Bowie knives. Another large glass case contains a cornucopia of "old West" curios such as spurs, pistols, knives, bugle, rattlesnake, barb wire, leggings and more.


Historic Bar

The Historic Bar features a massive two-piece cherry wood bar, restored to its original splendor as testament to the grandeur of the era. It is one of the oldest bars still in existence in Texas. William Green, the building's original owner was a local character, remembered in part for making sure that buckets on the floor of the bar were full of beer for his horse when he rode inside -- and for the big kegs of root beer gracing the bar during prohibition.

Where once all the important citizens of Shiner gathered in the bar for smokes and spirits, we invite you to enjoy your favorite beverage and enjoy the historic atmosphere. In addition to mixed drinks, beer, and wine, we are pleased to serve 45 guests in the bar from our full dining menu.


Spacious Banquet Room

The area that once was the meat market is now the banquet room, which seats 150 guests, with plenty of room for entertainment. The old barbecue pits are still in the back room.

The building, 88 years old and still going strong, is a dedication to past, present and future. "Some people go and recreate history, but we don't have to - it's already here."


A Brief Shiner History

In the valley between the Lavaca and Guadalupe rivers, it began as an Indian settlement called "Half Moon." It was a place of ranchers and cattle, the railroad and Indians, outlaws and Texas Rangers -- and cotton was "King." Texas roots run deep in Shiner, Texas.

George West, one of the first to drive cattle in this part of Texas, was married to a Shiner - and Henry B. Shiner donated the land for the town of Shiner to have its start in 1887.

William Green was an entrepreneur with interests in ranching, cotton, cattle and land development. In Wilson County, he purchased tracts of land, subdivided them and then sold smaller tracts. One of the first millionaires in the area, he and his wife Julia Dickson Green moved to Shiner in 1890, and the story goes that he had alligators in a pond in front of his house to keep unwanted guests away. Green was also the man who drove 500 horses from Shiner to San Antonio for Teddy Roosevelt and the RoughRiders.

In 1911, Green erected a 16,000 square-foot, two-story building on the main street. At the time, Shiner was peppered with bars - 31 by one account. And Green added the Palace Bar to the list. The bar was the hub of the town's activity, and with its rich, gleaming cherry wood furnishings, it was quite elegant for the time. Trading, deal making and business were conducted in the bar. The favored dish of the day was the "highball" - a concoction of half chili and half stew.

A pharmacy shared the downstairs area with the bar, and it sported lavish, ornate cherry wood 70-foot built-in cabinets with drawers and leaded glass. The second floor held Green's office, his grandson's office and offices for architects and doctors.

The original hospital for Shiner was on the second floor, with an impressive skylight in the roof for the surgery. One of the doctors commented that if a person could climb the stairs (28 steep, narrow ones), they weren't that sick.


Texas Preservation Award

In the '40s, Dickson Green sold the building to Hugo and Kamila Schwabe, who then sold it to Jimmy Mikes. In 1996, Russell and Roxanne Boothe purchased the building and have tried to restore it to its original distinction without changing any more than necessary. All of the wood -- ceilings and floors -- is the original. The Boothes changed the casual cafe and bar into a fine dining establishment, the Shiner Restaurant and Bar.

Texas First Lady Laura Bush visited Shiner in 1998 as part of a Main Street Program promotion. As she drove through downtown, she noticed the Green Building and suggested it be nominated as a historic site. And in 1999, the Boothes received the "Preservation Texas Clara Driscoll Award of Merit" for their preservation work on the Green Building. Local craftsmen and merchants who participated in the restoration project share the pride and recognition of this prestigious award.

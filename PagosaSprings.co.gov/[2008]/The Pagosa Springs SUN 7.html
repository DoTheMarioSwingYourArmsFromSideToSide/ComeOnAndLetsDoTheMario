Dick Moseley leads final 2008 wildflower walk
By Phyllis Wheaton

Special to The SUN

For several years, volunteer Dick Moseley has led a series of wildflower walks within the San Juan National Forest, and has gained quite a following.

Dick and his wife, Ann, are moving to Ohio to be nearer to family. His last walk for the season will be Tuesday, July 1. Those wishing to participate should meet at the Teal Boat Ramp prior to hiking the Cimarrona Trail.

Moseley worked in natural resources for 32 years, including 21 years as the head of the nature preserve and heritage programs for the state of Ohio.

Since his retirement, he has volunteered in many different roles for the San Juan National Forest, San Juan Mountains Association and Chimney Rock Interpretive Association. He will be sorely missed by many in the Forest Service and the community.

This last walk starts at 9 a.m. and is two to three hours long. Bring water, hat, sunscreen, repellant, a jacket and wear appropriate shoes. Dick will provide a plant list for the area. The program is free and is sponsored by the San Juan Mountains Association and San Juan National Forest.

For more information about this program and other interpretive events, contact Pagosa Ranger District at 264-2268.

FrIday, June 27, 2008
Construction to begin on coalbed methane gas wells in Sauls Creek area

By Ann Bond

Special to The SUN

The San Juan Public Lands Center will give final authorization to BP America Production Company early next week to drill four coalbed methane wells analyzed in the Environmental Impact Statement (EIS) process for the Northern San Juan Basin Coal Bed Methane Project. After five years of public involvement and study, the Record of Decision for the project was issued in April, 2007.

The four wells locations are in the Sauls Creek Area of the San Juan National Forest. The locations fall outside of the HD Mountain Roadless Area and away from the Fruitland Outcrop; two of the areas given the greatest attention in the development of the EIS. Construction activities are expected to begin as early as next week.

The Forest Service and BLM also anticipate giving approval to BP to construct up to seven other coal bed methane wells in the Sauls Creek area within the next one to two weeks. These well locations will also be located outside of the HD Mountain Roadless Area and away from the Fruitland Outcrop.

For more information, contact Walt Brown at 385-1372, or Matt Janowiak, 385-1378.

FrIday, June 27, 2008
 Shortage of travel vaccines

By Jane Looney

San Juan Basin Health

Special to The SUN

 If you’re planning an international trip this summer or early fall, you should go ahead and get your travel vaccinations now.

There is a current shortage of certain vaccine products, such as those for Japanese encephalitis, yellow fever and rabies (for pre-exposure). People traveling to China for the summer Olympics or to other Asian countries are those most likely to get vaccinated for Japanese encephalitis, whereas yellow fever areas are Africa and central South America. People traveling into remote areas of the world would sometimes get three doses of rabies vaccine which is given pre-exposure.  However, that vaccine is now restricted for individuals who have been injured by a potentially rabid animal.

 If you need any of the vaccines listed above, we suggest you call the San Juan Basin Health travel nurse as soon as possible at 247-5702, Ext. 271.

For all other travel consults and vaccines, call and schedule your appointment at least four to six weeks before your departure date. All travel consultations and vaccinations are done by appointment only.

For more information on travel vaccinations and background, visit www.sjbhd.org.

Wednesday, June 25, 2008
Town regulates political sign displays
With the current political season underway, and heating up for the impending primary and general elections, the Town of Pagosa Springs reminds residents of the regulations governing display of political signs within town boiundaries.

Political signs are allowed to be up to 6 square feet in size, per side.

Signs may be displayed in town starting 45 days prior to an election and must be removed within three days following the election.

Only one sign per candidate per lot is allowed, and signs must be located on private property, with the property owners’ permission.

No signs are allowed in the right of way (that area between a property line and a roadway).

A sign permit is not required for political signs.

For further information, call 264-4151, Ext. 225. Also, visit the Web page for more information — www.townofpagosasprings.com.

Dopha “Mildred” Levac
Dopha “Mildred” Levac, 89, passed away early Friday morning, June 20, 2008, at Coulee Community Hospital Nursing Home. She was born Nov. 4, 1918, to George Earl and Josie Belle Kingsley of Pagosa Springs, Colo. She grew up on a ranch and attended a one-room country school in Archuleta County, Colo., with her brothers and sisters.

She came to Grand Coulee in September 1942 with her first husband, George Crawford, Sr., and his son, George “Sonny” Crawford, Jr. They lived briefly in the Rose Bowl Cabins, dirt floors and all. Soon they moved to a modest rental house where they lived for nearly two years. Even though she thought Grand Coulee was the end of the earth, good friends assured her if she stayed six months, the place would grow on her, which proved true. At the end of WW II, she and George settled next door in their own home on King Street. Four years later, daughter Linda was born.

After her husband’s death in 1950, she chose to stay in Grand Coulee. She married Lionel “Leo” Levac of Massena, N.Y. in Coeur D’Alene, Idaho, on Aug. 4, 1951. They had two daughters, Pamela and Teresa.

Mildred’s life revolved around her family. She was a devoted mother who always knew where her children were and with whom they were spending their time. She was a dedicated room mother at the school, active in the PTA, Girl Scouts, and the Federated Women’s Club. She enjoyed visiting with her friends and family around the kitchen table. For her, the leading edge of technology was a new suds-saver washing machine, an electric iron, and a dial telephone without a party line.

She is survived by her husband Leo; three daughters: Linda (Richard) Black of Grand Coulee, Wash., Pamela Levac of Chino, Calif., Teresa (Mark) Nash of Spanaway, Wash.; five grandchildren, Douglas (Dawn), Steven (Semine), Amanda, and Joseph Nash of Spanaway, Wash.; Michelle (Scott) Savage of Chino, Calif.; four great grandchildren, Ashley and Anne Nash, and Chloe and Jacob (due in August) Savage. Numerous nieces and nephews also survive her. All nine of her siblings and her stepson, George Crawford, Jr., preceded her in death.

BoCC to consider community wildfire protection plan
The Archuleta County Community Wildfire Protection Plan (CWPP) has been under development for seven years. Many individuals and organizations have provided input, expertise and support during the process.

The Archuleta County Sheriff’s Office and various stakeholders are presenting the plan for approval during the public hearing section of the Archuleta County Board of County Commissioners’ meeting , at 1:30 p.m. Tuesday, June 24. This presentation will take place near the start of the meeting, to be held in the commission meeting room at the county courthouse.

In response to wildland fires in 2000 and 2002, the U.S. Congress authorized the National Fire Plan in 2000 and the Healthy Forests Restoration Act (HFRA) in 2003. Under this legislation, local communities are encouraged to develop and implement forest management plans and hazardous fuel reduction projects. U.S. Forest Service and Bureau of land Management are directed by HFRA to give consideration to the priorities of local communities as they develop and implement these plans.

In Archuleta County, this process started in 2001 with numerous participants developing the Community Fire Plan. This plan, developed as a component of the National Fire Plan, identified wildland fire risk areas and developed goals and action recommendations throughout Archuleta County. Five counties in southwest Colorado, including Archuleta County, unveiled Community Fire Plans, which collectively are considered national models for collaboration, and for intergovernmental planning and action around wildland fire education and emergency response.

The Archuleta County CWPP complements and builds upon the Community Fire Plan. It incorporates key principles and guidelines arising from the Healthy Forests Restoration Act. It is intended as an over arching document for the county, with the hope that individual communities will create CWPPs that are specific to their areas. The plan lays out an ambitious program for:

• Reducing wildland fire risk in the wildland-urban interface (WUI).

• Accomplishing important fuel treatments on federal and private lands.

• Reducing structural ignitability.

• Increasing community safety.

• Continuing successful education and community mobilization endeavors.

• Continuing to enhancing partnerships between federal, state and local agencies and among community organizations and local governments.

The plan also sets the Archuleta County WUI definition and defines Archuleta County’s Communities of Concern..

The draft plan is available at www.archuletacounty.org/oem/.

Contact the Sheriff’s Office of Emergency Management with any questions or comments at (970) 731-4799.

Tuesday, June 17, 2008
Searchers find bodies of missing snowboarders
Searchers Monday discovered the remains of Michael George and Kyle Kerschen, both 27 and both of Albuquerque — snowboarders who went missing at Wolf Creek Ski Area on Jan. 4.

Mineral County Sheriff Fred Hosselkus said Monday that George and Kerschen were found by members of an independent search team organized by the parents of the missing men.

According to Hosselkus, dogs being used by searchers working the “Knife Edge below Alberta Peak” caught a scent and searchers soon after discovered Kerschen.

Hosselkus said Davey Pitcher, of Wolf Creek Ski Area, was riding as the observer in a helicopter and spotted a red color in trees approximately a tenth of a mile from the site where Kerschen was found. The red was a jacket worn by George. “It was a pretty lucky find,” said Hosselkus.

The sheriff said the bodies were taken to Colorado springs for autopsy. The Mineral County Coroner was present at the autopsies. Hosselkus said , if need be, further identification would be done with DNA tests.

“I’m glad it’s over,” said the sheriff.

George and Kerschen were supposed to meet with family members in Pagosa Springs Jan. 3, and the pair never showed. On the morning of Jan. 4, ski area staff verified the snowboarders’ car was in the ski area parking lot, and the men were officially reported missing.

A multi-agency search was organized at the time, involving 50-60 individuals. Ground crews and helicopters were used in the search, with air efforts interrupted by weather and ground efforts undertaken in the face of severe weather, heavy snowfall and high avalanche danger.

Staff from Mineral, Rio Grande and Archuleta counties, the Forest Service, Wolf Creek Ski Area, Upper San Juan Search and Rescue, and volunteers from New Mexico participated in the initial search that included areas into which people often venture out of bounds.

Wednesday, June 11, 2008
LPEA to consider new federal standards
La Plata Electric Association, in response to changes made by the Energy Independence and Security Act of 2007 (EAct 2007), under the Public Utility Regulatory Act of 1978 (PURPA), Section 111, will conduct a public hearing at 9 a.m. Oct. 15, at the Durango headquarters in Bodo Park, 45 Stewart St.

In order to participate in the hearing, LPEA customers must notify LPEA by July 30, in writing, of their intention to do so and of the nature of their presentation.

Community members are invited to comment on the three new standards to be considered by LPEA, but only those who file a request to participate by July 30, 2008, will be permitted to present information at the public hearing.

“While La Plata Electric is a rural cooperative overseen by a board of directors, various aspects are also regulated by the state and federal governments,” said Greg Munro, LPEA CEO, citing PURPA and the new EAct 2007 as examples. “We must adhere to their requirements.”

LPEA and similar utility companies are required to consider adoption of the three new PURPA standards. The law does not, however, specify that utilities must adopt them.

The three PURPA standards under consideration and open to comment by LPEA member/customers are: 1)  the inclusion of energy efficiency into integrated resource plans; 2) the alignment of electric rates with the delivery of cost-effective energy efficiency as well as the promotion of energy efficiency investments; and, 3) the daily provision of direct written or electronic access to information concerning time-based electricity prices to all customers and information regarding the sources of power used, including greenhouse gas emissions. 

“This third consideration applies directly to Tri-State Generation & Transmission,” said Munro, reminding that LPEA is a distribution cooperative and purchases its full wholesale power requirements from Tri-State under a long-term, all-requirements contract. “LPEA does not directly own or control any generation resources, so we will look to Tri-State for their response to this PURPA standard. Member/customers may provide us comment on the third standard, which we will pass on directly to Tri-State.”

All LPEA customers are eligible to participate, but must notify LPEA in writing by mail, fax or e-mail by July 30. The notification must indicate name and address of the person requesting to participate; whether the person desires to make an oral presentation and if so, the anticipated length; and whether any special accommodations need to be made for the presenter. Written testimony or comments must be submitted to LPEA by Sept. 30, 2008, for consideration at the hearing.

A complete copy of the legislative language relevant to the PURPA standards is posted on LPEA’s Web site at www.lpea.coop. Any LPEA customer not able to access this web site and who desires a copy of the legislative language or testimonies should contact LPEA at 247-5786, by mail at P.O. Box 2750, Durango, CO 81302, or e-mail at purpa@lpea.coop, and a copy will be provided at cost. 

Determination of the standards is expected to be rendered on or before Nov. 19, 2008. For further information, contact LPEA at 247-5786.

LPEA candidate election packets available June 18
Election packets for those interested in running for a seat on the LPEA Board of Directors will be available June 18 at the LPEA offices in Pagosa Springs and Durango.

To be placed on the ballot, a candidate must return a completed packet to the Durango office by 10 a.m. on July 23.

Pursuant to LPEA bylaws, one-third of the director’s seats are up for election annually, one in each of LPEA’s four districts.

Incumbent directors seeking re-election for the three-year terms are Harry Cole (District 1-Archuleta County), Davin Montoya (District 2-south and west La Plata County), Jeff Berman (District 3-Durango proper) and Ed Zink (District 4-north and east La Plata County).

“LPEA is a cooperative, owned by and operated for the benefit of those who use its services,” said Greg Munro, LPEA CEO. “So, as you purchase your electricity from La Plata Electric, you are also a member/owner entitled to elect directors who determine company policy.”

Director candidates must be cooperative member/owners and be permanent residents of the district for which they are seeking office. Completed election petitions must contain 15 or more signatures from co-op members in a candidate’s district.

Ballots will be mailed to LPEA’s full membership on Aug. 22, and the results tallied during the LPEA Annual Meeting, set for Saturday, Sept. 13, at Bayfield High School.

Candidate packets contain additional election information and instructions, including copies of LPEA’s bylaws and board policies. Further information is available from Jeannie Bennett, 382-3505, or on the Web, www.lpea.coop.

“We do urge everyone to be involved in the cooperative, by serving as a director, or most certainly casting a vote,” said Munro. “The company exists to serve its members. Everyone has an economic interest and voice in LPEA’s future.”

LPEA, a Touchstone Energy Cooperative established in 1939, provides to its nearly 30,000 members with more than 40,000 meters, safe, reliable electricity at the lowest reasonable cost, while being environmentally responsible.

For additional information, contact LPEA at 247-5786 or visit www.lpea.coop.
